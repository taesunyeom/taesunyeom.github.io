On the Equivalence of SVM and MNI in Overparametrized Regime

The hard-margin support vector machine (SVM) and the minimum-norm interpolator (MNI) are usually introduced as two very different objects: the former is the canonical maximum-margin classifier, while the latter is the least-squares solution that a regression analysis would produce. A remarkable phenomenon in high-dimensional statistics is that, once the model is sufficiently overparametrized, these two solutions become exactly identical. This post explains why this happens, when it happens, and why it is useful.

Setup: Two Interpolating Solutions

Consider a binary classification dataset \(\{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n\) with \(\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and \(y_i \in \{\pm 1\}\), in the overparametrized regime \(d > n\) where the data matrix \(\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}\) has full row rank. We compare two estimators.

The hard-margin SVM solves

\[ \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w}} \|\mathbf{w}\|_2^2 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad y_i \, \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_i \geq 1 \;\; \forall i, \]

while the minimum-norm interpolator (equivalently, the minimum-\(\ell_2\)-norm solution of least squares on the labels) solves

\[ \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w}} \|\mathbf{w}\|_2^2 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_i = y_i \;\; \forall i, \]

which admits the closed form \(\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}} = \mathbf{X}^\top (\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^\top)^{-1} \mathbf{y}\).

Since \(y_i \in \{\pm 1\}\), the interpolation constraint \(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_i = y_i\) can be rewritten as \(y_i \, \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_i = 1\). In other words, the MNI is what one obtains by forcing every margin constraint of the SVM to hold with equality. This immediately gives a one-line relation between the two programs: the feasible set of the MNI is a subset of the feasible set of the SVM, so in general

\[ \|\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}}\|_2 \leq \|\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}}\|_2. \]

When Are They Equal?

The KKT conditions of the SVM state that the solution takes the form \(\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}} = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i \mathbf{x}_i\) with dual variables \(\alpha_i \geq 0\), and complementary slackness enforces \(\alpha_i > 0\) only if the corresponding margin constraint is active, i.e., \(y_i \, \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}}^\top \mathbf{x}_i = 1\). Points with active constraints are the support vectors.

The precise statement of the equivalence is the following [1, 2].

Proposition. Suppose \(d \geq n\) and \(\mathbf{X}\) has full row rank, and let \(\boldsymbol{\beta} = (\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^\top)^{-1}\mathbf{y}\). Then the following are equivalent:

  1. \(\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}} = \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}}\);

  2. every training point is a support vector, i.e., \(y_i \, \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}}^\top \mathbf{x}_i = 1\) for all \(i\);

  3. \(y_i \beta_i \geq 0\) for all \(i\).

Condition (3) says that each coefficient of the MNI, written in the basis of the training points, has the same sign as its label – so that it can serve as a valid (nonnegative) SVM dual variable. This is the form that is actually verified in the probabilistic analyses discussed in the next section.

Proof of the Proposition

(1) \(\Rightarrow\) (2). The MNI interpolates the labels by construction, so \(y_i \, \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}}^\top \mathbf{x}_i = y_i^2 = 1\) for all \(i\). If the two solutions coincide, every margin constraint of the SVM is therefore active.

(2) \(\Rightarrow\) (1). Recall from the feasible-set inclusion that \(\|\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}}\|_2 \leq \|\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}}\|_2\). If all margin constraints are active, then \(\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}}\) satisfies \(y_i \, \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}}^\top \mathbf{x}_i = 1\), i.e., it is feasible for the MNI program, which forces \(\|\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}}\|_2 \leq \|\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}}\|_2\). Hence both points minimize the strictly convex objective \(\|\mathbf{w}\|_2^2\) over the MNI constraint set, and by uniqueness of the minimizer they are equal.

(3) \(\Rightarrow\) (1). Write the MNI in the span of the training points:

\[ \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}} = \mathbf{X}^\top \boldsymbol{\beta} = \sum_i \beta_i \mathbf{x}_i = \sum_i (y_i \beta_i) \, y_i \mathbf{x}_i, \]

and set \(\alpha_i := y_i \beta_i\). We claim that \((\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}}, \boldsymbol{\alpha})\) satisfies the KKT conditions of the SVM: (stationarity) \(\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}} = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i \mathbf{x}_i\) holds by the display above; (primal feasibility) \(y_i \, \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}}^\top \mathbf{x}_i = 1 \geq 1\) since the MNI interpolates; (dual feasibility) \(\alpha_i \geq 0\) is exactly condition (3); (complementary slackness) holds trivially since every constraint is active. As the SVM is a convex program, the KKT conditions are sufficient for optimality, so \(\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}}\) solves the SVM, and by uniqueness \(\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}} = \hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}}\).

(1) \(\Rightarrow\) (3). By the KKT conditions, \(\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{SVM}} = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i \mathbf{x}_i\) for some \(\alpha_i \geq 0\). On the other hand, \(\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MNI}} = \sum_i \beta_i \mathbf{x}_i\). If the two coincide, then \(\sum_i (\alpha_i y_i - \beta_i) \, \mathbf{x}_i = \mathbf{0}\), and since \(\mathbf{X}\) has full row rank the vectors \(\{\mathbf{x}_i\}\) are linearly independent, so \(\beta_i = \alpha_i y_i\) for every \(i\). Multiplying by \(y_i\) gives \(y_i \beta_i = \alpha_i y_i^2 = \alpha_i \geq 0\). \(\square\)

At first glance condition (2) looks restrictive: in the classical low-dimensional picture, only a few points near the decision boundary are support vectors. The surprise is that in sufficiently high dimension, all points become support vectors.

Support Vector Proliferation

The phenomenon that every training point becomes a support vector is called support vector proliferation. The intuition is cleanest for isotropic features. When the entries of \(\mathbf{X}\) are i.i.d. with zero mean and unit variance, the Gram matrix concentrates as

\[ \mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^\top \approx d \, \mathbf{I}_n + \mathbf{E}, \qquad |\mathbf{E}_{ij}| = O(\sqrt{d}) \;\; \text{for } i \neq j, \]

so that \(\boldsymbol{\beta} = (\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^\top)^{-1}\mathbf{y} \approx \mathbf{y}/d\) and every \(y_i \beta_i \approx 1/d > 0\). In effect, when \(d \gg n\), the training points are nearly orthogonal to each other, and no single point can be expressed economically in terms of the others – so every point must participate in the SVM solution. We now turn this intuition into an actual proof, following the arguments of [1, 2]. Throughout, assume the features are isotropic Gaussian, \(\mathbf{x}_i \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_d)\), while the label \(y_i\) is allowed to depend on \(\mathbf{x}_i\) arbitrarily.

Step 1: A leave-one-out criterion

By the Proposition, it suffices to verify condition (3) coordinate-wise. Fix a point \(i\), and let \(\mathbf{X}_{-i}\) and \(\mathbf{y}_{-i}\) denote the data with point \(i\) removed, with \(\mathbf{A}_{-i} = \mathbf{X}_{-i}\mathbf{X}_{-i}^\top\). Permuting point \(i\) to the last position and applying the block (Schur complement) inversion formula to \(\boldsymbol{\beta} = (\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^\top)^{-1}\mathbf{y}\) gives

\[ \beta_i = \frac{y_i - \mathbf{x}_i^\top \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{-i}}{\|\mathbf{x}_i\|_2^2 - \mathbf{x}_i^\top \mathbf{X}_{-i}^\top \mathbf{A}_{-i}^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{-i} \mathbf{x}_i}, \qquad \text{where} \quad \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{-i} := \mathbf{X}_{-i}^\top \mathbf{A}_{-i}^{-1} \mathbf{y}_{-i}. \]

Two observations. First, \(\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{-i}\) is precisely the MNI trained on all points except \(i\): it is the leave-one-out predictor. Second, the denominator equals \(\|(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{P}_{-i})\mathbf{x}_i\|_2^2\), where \(\mathbf{P}_{-i}\) is the orthogonal projection onto the span of the other points; since \(d > n\), this is strictly positive almost surely. Hence the sign of \(\beta_i\) is the sign of the numerator, and multiplying by \(y_i\) (using \(y_i^2 = 1\)) yields the criterion

\[ y_i \beta_i > 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad y_i \, \mathbf{x}_i^\top \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{-i} < 1. \]

In words: point \(i\) is a support vector if and only if the interpolator built from the other \(n-1\) points does not already predict its label with margin at least \(1\). Support vector proliferation is thus the statement that no training point is redundant – no point is already explained by the rest of the data.

Step 2: Concentration in high dimension

It remains to show that the leave-one-out predictions \(g_i := \mathbf{x}_i^\top \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{-i}\) are uniformly small. The key point is independence: since the samples are i.i.d., \(\mathbf{x}_i\) is independent of \((\mathbf{X}_{-i}, \mathbf{y}_{-i})\), and hence of \(\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{-i}\). Conditioning on the latter,

\[ g_i \mid (\mathbf{X}_{-i}, \mathbf{y}_{-i}) \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\big(0, \|\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{-i}\|_2^2\big), \qquad \|\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{-i}\|_2^2 = \mathbf{y}_{-i}^\top \mathbf{A}_{-i}^{-1} \mathbf{y}_{-i} \leq \frac{n-1}{\lambda_{\min}(\mathbf{A}_{-i})}. \]

Standard bounds on the extreme singular values of Gaussian matrices give \(\lambda_{\min}(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^\top) \geq (\sqrt{d} - C\sqrt{n})^2 \geq d/2\) with probability at least \(1 - e^{-cd}\) once \(d \gtrsim n\); by Cauchy's interlacing theorem, the same lower bound holds simultaneously for every principal submatrix \(\mathbf{A}_{-i}\). On this event, \(\|\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{-i}\|_2^2 \leq 2n/d\) for all \(i\): the leave-one-out predictor has tiny norm, because it must interpolate \(n-1\) nearly orthogonal points of unit label size. The Gaussian tail bound then gives, for each \(i\),

\[ \mathbb{P}\big( y_i g_i \geq 1 \big) \;\leq\; \mathbb{P}\big( |g_i| \geq 1 \big) \;\leq\; 2\exp\!\Big( -\frac{1}{2\|\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{-i}\|_2^2} \Big) \;\leq\; 2\exp\!\Big( -\frac{d}{4n} \Big), \]

where taking the absolute value conveniently sidesteps the dependence between \(y_i\) and \(\mathbf{x}_i\). Finally, a union bound over the \(n\) points:

\[ \mathbb{P}\big( \exists i: \; y_i \beta_i \leq 0 \big) \;\leq\; 2n \exp\!\Big( -\frac{d}{4n} \Big) + e^{-cd} \;=\; 2\exp\!\Big( \log n -\frac{d}{4n} \Big) + e^{-cd}, \]

which vanishes as soon as \(d \geq 5 n \log n\). Thus \(d \gtrsim n \log n\) implies SVM \(=\) MNI with high probability. Note where the logarithmic factor entered: each individual point fails to be a support vector with probability \(e^{-\Theta(d/n)}\), and we pay a factor of \(n\) to control all points simultaneously.

Step 3: Sharpness of the threshold

The union bound is not wasteful here. The variables \(g_i\) are nearly independent, each approximately \(\mathcal{N}(0, n/d)\), and the maximum of \(n\) independent Gaussians concentrates around \(\sqrt{2\log n}\) times the standard deviation:

\[ \max_i \, y_i g_i \;\approx\; \sqrt{\frac{2 n \log n}{d}}. \]

This quantity crosses the critical value \(1\) precisely at \(d \asymp n \log n\). Below this scaling, some point is predicted by the others with margin exceeding \(1\), that point fails to be a support vector, and SVM \(\neq\) MNI. Making this heuristic rigorous requires handling the dependence among the \(g_i\); this was done by Ardeshir et al. [3], who established a sharp phase transition around \(d \asymp n \log n\) for isotropic Gaussian features: above this scaling the two solutions coincide with high probability, and below it they differ with high probability. The logarithmic factor is therefore not an artifact of the proof technique – it is real.

Beyond the isotropic case, Hsu et al. [2] extended the sufficient condition to anisotropic covariances (phrased in terms of effective dimensions), and Wang and Thrampoulidis [4] established support vector proliferation for Gaussian mixture data, where labels and features are dependent.

Why Does This Matter?

The equivalence is far more than a curiosity; it has become a standard workhorse in the theory of benign overfitting, for two main reasons.

Classification analysis reduces to regression analysis

The max-margin SVM is defined variationally and is generally hard to analyze directly, whereas the MNI has an explicit closed form amenable to random matrix analysis. Once SVM \(=\) MNI, any risk analysis of the minimum-norm least-squares interpolator – such as the benign overfitting characterization of Bartlett et al. [5] – can be imported to study the classification error of the SVM. This is precisely the route taken by Muthukumar et al. [1], who showed that in certain overparametrized regimes the interpolating classifier achieves vanishing classification risk even in settings where the corresponding regression risk does not vanish: classification is, in this sense, easier than regression.

The loss function does not matter

Recall the implicit bias result of Soudry et al. [6]: gradient descent on separable data with the logistic (or any exponential-tail) loss converges in direction to the hard-margin SVM. Combining this with the equivalence above yields a striking conclusion for the overparametrized regime: training a linear classifier with the logistic loss (run to convergence) and simply performing minimum-norm least-squares regression on the \(\pm 1\) labels produce the same classifier direction. In the heavily overparametrized regime, the choice between the two most common training losses – cross-entropy and squared loss – is immaterial to the solution found, which is exactly the message of the title of [1].
